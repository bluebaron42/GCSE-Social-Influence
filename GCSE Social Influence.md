Conformity: Asch's study

The specification says...
Conformity: Asch's study of conformity.

Ancient astrologers believed that there was a flow of an ethereal (other worldly) fluid from the stars which surrounds us all, affecting human actions and destinies. This fluid was called "influentia".

The word 'influence' comes from this. Social influences, like influentia, cannot be seen but we feel them and they are very powerful.

You can experience the power of these influences yourself: Imagine going up to someone on an underground train or in a café and say 'Excuse me, can I have your seat?'.

You will probably find it very hard to do this simple thing. The other person might say 'no' but so what? The other person won't shout at you or harm you. So what stops you? It can be explained in terms of social influences — the unseen pressure from others that makes us act in some predictable ways.

What is meant by 'conformity'?

Conformity is a form of social influence. It occurs when a person's behaviour or thinking changes as a result of group pressure. This 'pressure' from others may be real or imagined and can come from just one person or a group of people.

Stretch and challenge
Asch interviewed the naive participants after the study finished and asked them why they had conformed to an obviously wrong answer. They said they conformed because they didn't want to be disliked by the group.

This is one explanation for why we tend to behave like everyone else - so we can fit in. This is called normative social influence.

Another reason why people conform is because they are not sure what to do and think others know better. This is called informational social influence.

Asch's study

The classic research into conformity was conducted by Solomon Asch in the 1950s.

Aims
Asch wanted to investigate how people would respond to group pressure. He wanted to see if people would conform to the majority in a situation where the answer was obvious.

Method
Asch used 123 American male students. Each participant was tested individually with a group of between 6 and 8 confederates (people who were 'in' on the experiment). The participant was not aware of this and thought the other people were participants like himself.

On each trial, every person in the group had to say which of three lines A, B or C (the comparison lines) was the same as the test line (X). The answer was always obvious.

The genuine participant was always the last or next to last to answer.

Each participant took part in 18 trials. On 12 of these (the 'critical trials'), the confederates were instructed to give the same incorrect answer.

Results
Asch found that on the 12 critical trials the participants gave the wrong answer 36.8% of the time (they conformed). 75% of the participants conformed at least once. 25% of the participants never conformed.

Conclusions
The results show that people conform to a majority even when the majority is wrong. However, it is also important to remember that most people did not conform.

Evaluation

A child of its time
One weakness is that Asch's study was conducted in the 1950s in America. This was a time when people were very conformist and didn't want to be different from others. In another study (Perrin and Spencer 1980) only one student conformed in a total of 396 trials. This suggests that Asch's results may have been specific to that time and may not be true of people today.

An artificial task
A further weakness is that the task was artificial. Asking people to judge line lengths is not something they would do in everyday life. The people in the group were also strangers. In real life we usually conform in groups of people we know. This means that Asch's study might not tell us much about conformity in everyday situations.

Cultural differences
Another weakness is that the study was only conducted with American men. Other research has shown that people in countries like China are more likely to conform than people in countries like the UK or USA. This is because China has a more collectivist culture (where the group is more important than the individual). This suggests that Asch's findings may not be the same for all cultures.

Social factors affecting conformity

The specification says...
Social factors affecting conformity: group size, anonymity and task difficulty.

Asch found that people would conform to an obviously wrong answer. However, in other versions of his study he found that certain factors made people more or less likely to conform. These are known as social factors.

Group size

Asch wanted to know whether the size of the group would be more important than the agreement of the group.

He found that with two confederates, conformity to the wrong answer was 13.6%. When there were three confederates, it rose to 31.8%. Adding more confederates (up to 15) made very little difference.

Conclusion
The size of the majority does affect conformity but only up to a point.

Anonymity

In his original study the participants had to say their answer out loud. This meant everyone in the group knew what they had said.

Asch did another version where the participant could write their answer down. This meant it was anonymous. He found that conformity decreased when the participant could write their answer down.

Conclusion
If we don't think other people in the group will know what we have said, we feel less pressure to conform to their opinion.

Task difficulty

In Asch's original study the difference between the lines was very clear. He made the task more difficult by making the comparison lines much more similar to each other in length.

He found that when the task was more difficult, conformity increased.

Conclusion
When we are not sure of the answer we look to others for help. This is an example of informational social influence.

Evaluation

Group size
One weakness of the 'group size' factor is that it depends on the type of task. In Asch's study there was an obvious right answer. When there is no obvious right answer, group size becomes more important. For example, if you are asked which of two music tracks you prefer, the more people who say they like one track, the more likely you are to agree. This suggests that group size doesn't affect conformity in the same way in all situations.

Anonymity
A weakness of the 'anonymity' factor is that Asch used a group of strangers. Other research has found that if you are in a group of friends, anonymity makes very little difference to conformity. In fact, people might be even more likely to conform if they can write their answer down because they don't want to look 'different' to their friends. This suggests that the effect of anonymity depends on who you are with.

Task difficulty
One strength of the 'task difficulty' factor is that it has been supported by other research. Lucas et al. (2006) asked students to give answers to mathematical problems that were easy or more difficult. They found that there was more conformity to incorrect answers when the problems were difficult. This shows that Asch was correct that task difficulty is a factor that affects conformity.

Dispositional factors affecting conformity

The specification says...
Dispositional factors affecting conformity: personality, expertise.

Social factors are things in the environment that can affect whether we conform or not. However, not everyone in Asch's study conformed. This suggests that there must be other factors involved. These are called dispositional factors — factors that are part of our personality or our character.

Personality

Psychologists have suggested that some people have a more 'conformist' personality than others.

One characteristic that has been studied is locus of control. Locus of control refers to how much control a person feels they have over their own life.

People with an internal locus of control believe they are responsible for what happens to them. People with an external locus of control believe that what happens to them is down to luck or other outside forces.

Conclusion
People with an internal locus of control are less likely to conform because they have more confidence in their own opinions.

Expertise

Expertise refers to how much a person knows about a particular subject.

If you are an expert in something you are less likely to look to other people for the answer. For example, a study asked people to judge the value of some antiques. People who were experts in antiques were less likely to conform to the opinions of others than people who were not experts.

Conclusion
People with high levels of expertise in a task are less likely to conform.

Evaluation

Locus of control
A weakness of the locus of control explanation is that it doesn't always predict who will conform. In some situations, people with an internal locus of control still conform. For example, if they want to be liked by the group (normative social influence), they might still go along with the majority even if they think they are wrong. This suggests that personality is only one factor that affects conformity.

Expertise
A weakness of the expertise factor is that it can be affected by other factors. For example, even if you are an expert in something, you might still conform if you are in a group of people you really admire. This means that expertise alone doesn't always prevent conformity.

Obedience: Milgram's study

The specification says...
Obedience: Milgram's study of obedience.

Obedience is a form of social influence. It occurs when a person follows a direct order from another person, usually someone in a position of authority.

Milgram's study

Stanley Milgram (1963) wanted to know why so many people in Nazi Germany had followed orders to kill millions of people. He wanted to see if ordinary people would obey an order from an authority figure even if it meant hurting an innocent person.

Aims
To investigate how far people would go in obeying an order if it involved harming another person.

Method
Milgram used 40 American men between the ages of 20 and 50. They were recruited through a newspaper advert and were paid to take part.

The study took place at Yale University. The participant was introduced to another 'participant' (who was actually a confederate). They drew lots to see who would be the 'teacher' and who would be the 'learner'. This was rigged so that the genuine participant was always the teacher.

There was also an 'experimenter' (another confederate) who wore a grey lab coat.

The learner was taken into a room and strapped into a chair with electrodes. The teacher (the participant) was taken into a separate room which contained a 'shock generator'.

The teacher was told to give the learner an electric shock every time he gave the wrong answer on a memory task. The shocks started at 15 volts and went up in 15-volt steps to 450 volts.

The learner was not actually shocked, but the teacher didn't know this. At 300 volts, the learner kicked the wall and then stopped answering.

If the teacher didn't want to continue, the experimenter gave a series of 'prods' such as 'Please continue' or 'The experiment requires that you continue'.

Results
Milgram found that all participants went up to 300 volts. 65% of the participants went all the way to 450 volts (the maximum).

Conclusions
The results show that ordinary people will obey an order from an authority figure even if it means hurting an innocent person.

Evaluation

A lack of realism
One weakness is that the participants may not have believed the shocks were real. Although Milgram said they were, some researchers have suggested that the participants were just 'playing along' with the study. This would mean that the study doesn't really tell us anything about obedience in real life.

Ethical issues
A major weakness is that the study was unethical. The participants were deceived (they were told the shocks were real) and they were not protected from harm (many of them were very distressed during the study). This has led many people to criticize Milgram for his lack of care for the participants.

Supported by other research
A strength is that the study has been supported by other research. For example, a study with nurses (Hofling et al. 1966) found that 21 out of 22 nurses obeyed an order from an unknown doctor to give a patient a dangerous dose of a drug. This shows that Milgram's findings can be applied to real-life settings.

Agency theory

The specification says...
Explanations for obedience: agency theory.

Milgram developed a theory to explain why people obey. This is called agency theory.

Agentic state and autonomous state

Milgram suggested that we can be in one of two 'states'.

The autonomous state is when we feel responsible for our own actions. We choose how to behave and we feel the consequences of those actions.

The agentic state is when we feel we are acting as an 'agent' for someone else. We don't feel responsible for our actions because we believe we are just following orders.

Agentic shift

The move from being in an autonomous state to an agentic state is called the agentic shift. This happens when we are in the presence of an authority figure.

Moral strain

Even when people are in an agentic state they might still feel that what they are doing is wrong. This is called moral strain. This explains why the participants in Milgram's study were so distressed — they were in an agentic state but they still felt bad about what they were doing.

Evaluation

Research support
One strength of agency theory is that it is supported by Milgram's own research. When he interviewed participants after the study, they often said they only did it because they were told to. This suggests they were in an agentic state and didn't feel responsible for their actions.

Doesn't explain all obedience
A weakness of agency theory is that it doesn't explain why some people don't obey. In Milgram's study, 35% of the participants refused to go all the way to 450 volts. According to agency theory, all of them should have been in an agentic state and obeyed. This suggests that there are other factors involved in obedience, such as personality.

Authoritarian personality

The specification says...
Explanations for obedience: authoritarian personality.

Another explanation for obedience is that some people have a personality that makes them more likely to obey. This is called the Authoritarian Personality.

This theory was developed by Adorno et al. (1950). They suggested that people with this personality type have certain characteristics:

They are very respectful of authority.

They are very submissive to those in power.

They are very hostile to those they see as 'inferior' or different.

They have very rigid and traditional ideas about right and wrong.

How the personality develops

Adorno suggested that the authoritarian personality develops in childhood as a result of harsh parenting. If parents are very strict and only show love when the child is obedient, the child grows up with a strong sense of respect for authority.

However, the child also feels a lot of anger towards their parents but they can't express it. Instead, they 'displace' this anger onto people they see as weaker or different (such as minority groups).

Evaluation

Research support
One strength is that there is research to support the link between an authoritarian personality and obedience. Elms and Milgram (1966) interviewed some of the participants from Milgram's study. They found that the participants who were highly obedient also scored highly on a test of authoritarianism. This suggests that personality is a factor in obedience.

A limited explanation
A weakness is that it is hard to explain how a whole country could have an authoritarian personality. For example, in Nazi Germany millions of people obeyed orders. It is unlikely that all of them had the same type of personality and had the same harsh upbringing. This suggests that social factors (such as the situation) are more important than personality in explaining obedience on a large scale.

Situational factors affecting obedience

The specification says...
Situational factors affecting obedience: proximity, location and uniform.

As well as personality and the agentic state, Milgram found that certain features of the environment affected how likely people were to obey. These are known as situational factors.

Proximity

Proximity refers to how close the teacher was to the learner and how close the teacher was to the experimenter.
In one version of the study, the teacher and learner were in the same room. Obedience fell from 65% to 40%. When the teacher had to force the learner's hand onto a 'shock plate', obedience fell even further to 30%.
In another version, the experimenter gave his orders over the telephone. Obedience fell to 20.5%.

Conclusion
The closer we are to the consequences of our actions, the less likely we are to obey an order that harms someone.

Location

The original study took place at Yale University, which is a very prestigious and respected institution.
Milgram moved the study to a run-down office building in a nearby town. Obedience fell from 65% to 47.5%.

Conclusion
The status of the location gives the experimenter more authority, making it more likely that people will obey.

Uniform

In the original study, the experimenter wore a grey lab coat which gave him a high-status appearance.
In one version, the experimenter was called away and his place was taken by an 'ordinary member of the public' (another confederate) in everyday clothes. Obedience fell to 20%.

Conclusion
Uniforms act as a strong visual symbol of authority and increase obedience.

Evaluation

Research support
One strength is that there is research support for the effect of uniform. Bickman (1974) asked people in New York to do things like pick up litter or give a coin for a parking meter. People were twice as likely to obey someone dressed as a security guard than someone dressed in a jacket and tie. This supports Milgram's conclusion that uniform increases obedience.

Cross-cultural support
Another strength is that these findings have been supported in other cultures. Miranda et al. (1981) found an obedience rate of over 90% among Spanish students. This suggests that Milgram's findings are not just true of American men and can be applied to other cultures and genders.

Minority influence

The specification says...
Pro-social behaviour: minority influence.

Most of the time we think of social influence as the majority influencing the minority. However, sometimes a small group of people (a minority) can influence a larger group (the majority). This is how social change happens — for example, the suffragettes were a minority who eventually changed the views of the majority about women's right to vote.

Moscovici's study (1969)

Serge Moscovici wanted to see if a consistent minority could influence a majority.

Aims
To investigate the effect of a consistent minority on a majority in a situation where the answer was clear.

Method
Groups of six people were shown 36 blue-coloured slides that varied in intensity. They had to say the colour of each slide out loud.
In each group there were two confederates (the minority) and four genuine participants (the majority).
There were two conditions:

In the consistent condition, the two confederates said that all 36 slides were green.

In the inconsistent condition, the confederates said that 24 slides were green and 12 were blue.

Results
In the consistent condition, the genuine participants gave the same wrong answer as the minority on 8.42% of the trials. 32% gave the same answer at least once.
In the inconsistent condition, the agreement fell to 1.25%.

Conclusions
A minority can influence a majority, but they are much more likely to do so if they are consistent in their views.

Factors that make a minority successful

Consistency: If the minority all say the same thing and keep saying it over a long period of time, it makes the majority stop and think.

Commitment: If the minority show they are willing to suffer for their views (for example, by going to prison), it shows the majority how important the views are.

Flexibility: If the minority are too rigid and refuse to listen to any other views, they can be seen as dogmatic. Being prepared to compromise makes them more successful.

Crowd and collective behaviour

The specification says...
Pro-social and anti-social behaviour: crowd and collective behaviour.

Collective behaviour occurs when a large group of people (a crowd) join together in a common activity. This can be pro-social (positive and helpful, like a charity event) or anti-social (harmful and destructive, like a riot).

Deindividuation

Psychologists have suggested that being in a crowd changes our behaviour because we lose our sense of individual identity. This is called deindividuation.
When we are in a crowd, we feel anonymous and less likely to be identified. This means we feel less responsible for our actions and are more likely to follow the norms of the crowd, even if they are anti-social.

Reicher's study (1984)

Stephen Reicher wanted to investigate the behaviour of a crowd during a riot in St Pauls, Bristol, in 1980.

Method
Reicher analyzed news reports, photographs and interviewed people who had been at the riot.

Results
He found that the rioting was not random. It was directed at specific targets, mainly the police and property that represented authority (such as a bank). People living in the area were not attacked.
Once the police had left the area, the rioting stopped and the atmosphere became much more positive.

Conclusions
The results show that even in a riot, crowd behaviour is not completely lawless. The crowd follows the norms of the group they belong to. This challenges the idea that being in a crowd always leads to mindless violence.

Evaluation

Research support for deindividuation
One strength is that there is research to support the idea of deindividuation. Zimbardo (1969) found that participants who were made to feel anonymous (by wearing hoods and being in the dark) were more likely to give higher electric shocks to another person than participants who were identifiable. This supports the idea that losing your identity makes you more likely to act anti-socially.

A positive side to crowds
A weakness of the deindividuation theory is that it focuses too much on anti-social behaviour. Not all crowds are violent. For example, at a music festival or a religious event, the crowd can be very pro-social and helpful. This suggests that being in a crowd doesn't always lead to a loss of control.

Crowd and collective behaviour: Social and dispositional factors

The specification says...
Social and dispositional factors affecting crowd and collective behaviour.

As we have seen, being in a crowd can affect our behaviour. However, there are also other factors that determine how a crowd behaves.

Social factors

Social factors are things in the environment or the situation.

Social loafing: This occurs when people put in less effort when they are working in a group than when they are working alone. This is because they feel their individual effort cannot be identified. For example, in a tug-of-war, people pull less hard if they are in a team than if they are on their own.

Culture: Some cultures are more likely to work together than others. In collectivist cultures (like China), people are less likely to show social loafing because they value the success of the group more than the individual.

Dispositional factors

Dispositional factors are part of our personality.

Personality: People with a high level of self-esteem are less likely to be influenced by the crowd because they have more confidence in their own values.

Morality: People with a strong sense of right and wrong are less likely to follow a crowd if they think what the crowd is doing is immoral.

Group work
Group work is common in schools and it also contributes to creative tasks.
One study looked at participants working on 'eureka' type problems such as the Five disc puzzle. Four-person groups were able to solve such problems faster than individuals (Shaw 1932). 'Brainstorming' is an example of this approach where a group can generate more ideas than individuals working alone.
